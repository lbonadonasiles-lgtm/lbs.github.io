Project Overview

Engineering for Ruby

Led a team of 6 Biomedical Engineering students to adapt a commercial ride-on toy vehicle for Ruby, a child diagnosed with Angelman syndrome — a neurogenetic condition that caused limited lower-limb motor control and difficulty maintaining an upright posture. Our goal was to restore the experience of independent, self-directed movement that most children take for granted.

Working directly with clinicians at the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences (UAMS), we translated medical mobility requirements into actionable engineering specifications. Every modification was designed with Ruby's specific needs in mind, and every material choice was made with one goal: keep the total build cost under $600, so that any family facing a similar situation could replicate it at home.

Why It Matters

A Gap in Coverage

In the United States, health insurance — including Medicaid — does not cover powered mobility devices for children under the age of three. The rationale is that independent mobility is not yet considered a medical necessity at that developmental stage. For families of children with motor impairments, however, the consequences of this policy are significant: commercial adaptive mobility devices often carry price tags ranging from $15,000 to over $30,000, placing them entirely out of reach for most households.

Research consistently shows that early independent mobility has profound effects on a child's cognitive, social, and emotional development. Children who can explore their environment on their own terms develop language skills faster, form stronger social connections, and demonstrate better spatial reasoning. The inability to move independently is not merely a physical limitation — it reshapes how a child experiences and interacts with the world during the most formative years of their development.

The Go Baby Go! program was founded on the premise that these outcomes should not be determined by a family's financial circumstances. By modifying off-the-shelf commercial ride-on toy vehicles — available at major retailers for under $100 — into functional adaptive mobility devices, the program delivers that developmental access at a fraction of the cost.

Technical Work

The Modifications

01

Push-Button Drive Conversion

Redesigned the vehicle's drive system by redirecting power from the factory foot-pedal actuator to a single push-button mounted on the steering wheel. This allowed Ruby to operate the vehicle independently using only her hand, without requiring any lower-limb involvement.

02

3D-Printed Control Mount

Designed and 3D-printed a custom button housing that mounts directly to the steering wheel and integrates PVC pipe extensions, reducing the reach distance required and making the controls accessible and ergonomic for a young child with limited range of motion.

03

Parent Emergency Stop

Rewired the vehicle's electrical circuit to incorporate a dedicated emergency stop button mounted at the rear of the vehicle, accessible at all times by the caregiver. The override cuts power immediately, ensuring a reliable safety mechanism during any unexpected situation.

04

Bluetooth Audio Integration

Integrated a microcontroller with Bluetooth connectivity and an onboard speaker, allowing Ruby to stream her favorite music wirelessly during rides. The addition was designed to enhance sensory engagement and make each session more enjoyable and stimulating.

05

Custom License Plate

Laser engraved a personalized "RUBY" license plate for the vehicle. A small detail — but one that gives the device a sense of identity and ownership, making it unmistakably hers.

06

Reproducibility Manual

Authored a detailed step-by-step build guide — including a complete materials list under $600 — for families who were not selected for the Go Baby Go! program. The manual is written for a non-technical audience, enabling parents to replicate every modification independently at home.
